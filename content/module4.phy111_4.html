Part 4: Waves, Electricity, and Modern Physics

Chapter 22: Wave Motion

This chapter introduces the fundamental concepts of wave motion. It begins by defining a propagating wave as a self-sustaining disturbance in a medium that travels from one point to another, carrying energy and momentum. The key distinction is made between transverse waves (like those on a string, where particle vibration is perpendicular to propagation direction) and longitudinal waves (like sound, where particle vibration is parallel to propagation).

Important terminology is defined, including:

  • Wavelength (λ): The distance between corresponding points on successive waves.
  • Frequency (f): The number of cycles per second (Hz).
  • Period (T): The time for one complete cycle (T = 1/f).
  • Speed (v): The speed of wave propagation (v = fλ).

The chapter then delves into standing waves, which occur when a system resonates efficiently at certain vibrational frequencies. Nodes and antinodes are explained, as is the condition for resonance. Finally, the chapter concludes with a discussion of longitudinal waves in air columns and solid bars.

  1. Describes how to calculate the speed of a transverse wave on a string.
  2. Explains how to find the frequency of a transverse wave on a string given its tension, length, and mass per unit length.
  3. Covers calculation of sound wave speed and fundamental frequency.
  4. Explores harmonic frequencies of string and open and closed pipes.

Chapter 23: Sound

This chapter focuses on sound, a type of longitudinal wave. It discusses:

  • Speed of sound: The speed of sound in ideal gases and its dependence on temperature and molecular mass is derived.
  • Intensity of sound: Defined as energy per unit area per unit time, and its relationship to loudness (decibels).
  • Loudness (or sound level): The logarithmic scale for measuring sound intensity.
  • Beats: The phenomenon of alternating loudness and softness caused by two waves with slightly different frequencies.
  • Doppler effect: The apparent change in frequency due to relative motion between the source and observer.
  • Interference effects: The combination of two sound waves and its dependence on path differences.

Chapter 24: Coulomb's Law and Electric Fields

This chapter introduces electrostatics. Key topics include:

  • Coulomb's Law: The force between two point charges is discussed, including the dependence on the magnitude of charges and the distance between them. The role of the Coulomb constant (k) and the permittivity of free space (ε₀) are examined. The concept of quantized charge (the existence of elementary charge, e) is introduced.
  • Electric Fields: The concept of an electric field as a force per unit charge is explained. The chapter explains how to calculate electric field strength at various points due to one or more point charges using the principle of superposition.
  • Conservation of charge: The principle that charge can neither be created nor destroyed in isolated systems is discussed.

Chapter 25: Potential; Capacitance

This chapter builds on Coulomb's Law and electric fields by introducing:

  • Potential difference: Defined as the work done per unit charge to move a charge between two points. The relationship to voltage is explained.
  • Absolute potential: The potential at a point relative to infinity is described.
  • Capacitance: The ability of a system to store charge is examined.
  • Parallel-plate capacitors: The capacitance of a parallel-plate capacitor is defined and equations for its calculation are given, including the effect of dielectric materials.
  • Capacitors in series and parallel: The effective capacitance of multiple capacitors connected in series and parallel are shown.
  • Energy stored in a capacitor: The energy stored in a capacitor is derived.

Chapter 26: Current, Resistance, and Ohm's Law

This chapter introduces the fundamentals of electric circuits:

  • Electric current: Defined as the rate of flow of charge. The direction of conventional current is discussed.
  • Resistance: Defined as the ratio of potential difference to current.
  • Ohm's Law: The relationship between voltage, current, and resistance is explained. The concept of resistivity is introduced.
  • Resistivity: The resistance of a material per unit length and cross-sectional area.
  • Temperature dependence of resistance: How resistance changes with temperature is covered.
  • Terminal potential difference: The potential difference across the terminals of a battery or generator, considering internal resistance.

Chapter 27: Electrical Power

This chapter deals with the energy and power aspects of electric circuits:

  • Electrical work: Defined as the product of charge and potential difference.
  • Electrical power: Defined as the rate at which electrical work is done.
  • Power loss in a resistor: The power dissipated (heat generated) in a resistor is derived. The relationship between power, current, voltage, and resistance is described.
  • Thermal energy generated in a resistor: The relationship between power loss and heat generated is detailed.
  • Convenient energy conversions: The chapter provides conversion factors between various units of power and energy.

Chapter 28: Equivalent Resistance; Simple Circuits

This chapter focuses on simplifying and analyzing simple circuits:

  • Resistors in series: The equivalent resistance of resistors connected in series is derived.
  • Resistors in parallel: The equivalent resistance of resistors connected in parallel is derived.
  • Simple circuit analysis: Techniques for finding equivalent resistance and currents in simple circuits are illustrated using both series and parallel combinations of resistors, and also more complex combinations.

Chapter 29: Kirchhoff's Laws

This chapter introduces Kirchhoff's laws, powerful tools for analyzing complex circuits:

  • Kirchhoff's node (or junction) rule: The sum of currents entering a node equals the sum of currents leaving.
  • Kirchhoff's loop (or circuit) rule: The algebraic sum of potential drops around any closed loop is zero.
  • Solving circuit problems using Kirchhoff's laws: The chapter demonstrates how to apply Kirchhoff's laws to solve for unknown currents and voltages in complex circuits, often involving multiple voltage sources and loops.

Chapter 30: Forces in Magnetic Fields

The chapter introduces the interaction between magnetic fields and moving charges:

  • Magnetic fields: The concept of a magnetic field is presented and how to represent magnetic fields using field lines.
  • Force on a moving charge: The force on a charge moving through a magnetic field is derived (Lorentz force), including the role of velocity, charge, field strength, and the angle between velocity and field. The right-hand rule is used to determine the force's direction.
  • Magnetic field lines: Visual representation of magnetic fields and their direction.
  • Force on a current: The force on a current-carrying wire in a magnetic field is discussed, showing how to calculate the magnitude and direction of the force using the right-hand rule.
  • Torque on a flat coil: The torque on a current-carrying coil in a uniform magnetic field is derived.

Chapter 31: Sources of Magnetic Fields

This chapter explores how magnetic fields are generated:

  • Magnetic fields produced by moving charges: The chapter describes the relationship between moving charges and magnetic fields.
  • Magnetic field due to a long straight wire: The magnetic field produced by a long, straight current-carrying wire is discussed, and its dependence on current and distance from the wire is shown. The right-hand rule is used to find the field's direction.
  • Ferromagnetic materials: The chapter describes ferromagnetism and how it affects magnetic fields.
  • Magnetic moment: The magnetic moment of a current loop is defined.
  • Magnetic field contribution of a current element: The magnetic field produced by a small segment of a current-carrying wire is examined.

Chapter 32: Induced EMF; Magnetic Flux

This chapter covers electromagnetic induction:

  • Magnetic effects of matter: How matter interacts with magnetic fields is presented. Relative permeability is discussed.
  • Magnetic field lines: A visual representation of magnetic fields, useful for understanding flux.
  • Magnetic flux: Defined as the product of magnetic field strength and area, with consideration of the angle between field and area.
  • Induced EMF: Faraday's law of induction and Lenz's law, explaining how a changing magnetic flux induces an electromotive force in a loop of wire.
  • Motional EMF: EMF induced in a conductor moving through a magnetic field is discussed.

Chapter 33: Electric Generators and Motors

This chapter explores the practical applications of electromagnetic induction:

  • Electric generators: How generators convert mechanical energy into electrical energy is explained. The concept of back emf is introduced.
  • Electric motors: How motors convert electrical energy into mechanical energy is described. Back emf in a motor is explained.

Chapter 34: Self-Inductance; R-C and R-L Time Constants

This chapter delves into the behavior of inductors and capacitors in circuits:

  • Self-inductance: The ability of a coil to induce an emf in itself due to a changing current is explained. The concept of inductance (L) is introduced.
  • Mutual inductance: The ability of one coil to induce an emf in another is discussed.
  • Energy stored in an inductor: How energy is stored in an inductor's magnetic field.
  • R-C time constant: The time constant (RC) that describes how quickly a capacitor charges or discharges through a resistor.
  • R-L time constant: The time constant (L/R) that describes how quickly the current in an inductor changes when connected to a resistor.

Chapter 35: Alternating Current

This chapter covers circuits with alternating current (AC):

  • EMF generated by a rotating coil: The sinusoidal nature of AC voltage and current is explored.
  • Meters for AC circuits: How AC meters measure effective (rms) values of voltage and current.
  • Forms of Ohm's Law: Ohm's law in AC circuits, including the concept of impedance (Z).
  • Impedance (Z): The total opposition to current flow in an AC circuit, including resistance, inductive reactance, and capacitive reactance.
  • Resonance: The frequency at which inductive and capacitive reactances cancel, leading to maximum current.
  • Power loss in AC circuits: Power loss due to resistance and phase differences.
  • Transformers: Devices used to step up or step down AC voltages.

Chapter 36: Reflection of Light

This chapter begins the optics section with reflection:

  • Nature of light: A brief discussion of the nature of light as an electromagnetic wave, but emphasizes that the wave model is sufficient for the problems in the chapter.
  • Law of reflection: The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection.
  • Plane mirrors: Image formation by plane mirrors and how to construct ray diagrams.
  • Spherical mirrors: Image formation by concave and convex spherical mirrors is discussed, and equations are derived for their focal lengths and magnification.
  • Mirror equation: A formula relating object distance, image distance, and focal length.

Chapter 37: Refraction of Light

This chapter covers refraction:

  • Speed of light: How the speed of light varies depending on the medium.
  • Index of refraction: Defined as the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum to the speed of light in a material.
  • Refraction: The bending of light as it passes from one medium to another. Snell's law is introduced.
  • Critical angle for total internal reflection: The conditions for total internal reflection are explained.
  • Prisms: Dispersion of light by prisms.

Chapter 38: Thin Lenses

This chapter discusses lenses:

  • Types of lenses: Converging and diverging lenses are described. Focal lengths are defined.
  • Object and image relation: Equations relating object distance, image distance, and focal length for converging and diverging lenses.
  • Lensmaker's equation: A formula for calculating the focal length of a lens given its refractive index and radii of curvature.
  • Lens power: A measure of a lens's ability to converge or diverge light.
  • Lenses in contact: How to find the combined power of multiple lenses in contact.

Chapter 39: Optical Instruments

This chapter deals with the applications of lenses and mirrors:

  • Combination of thin lenses: How to locate the final image formed by multiple lenses.
  • Magnifying glass: Image formation and magnification.
  • Microscopes: Image formation and magnification of compound microscopes.
  • Telescopes: Image formation and magnification of telescopes.

Chapter 40: Interference and Diffraction of Light

This chapter explores wave phenomena in light:

  • Coherent waves: Waves with the same frequency and a fixed phase difference.
  • Interference: Constructive and destructive interference.
  • Diffraction: Bending of light around obstacles.
  • Single-slit diffraction: Diffraction pattern from a single slit.
  • Limit of resolution: The minimum angular separation required to distinguish two objects.
  • Diffraction gratings: How gratings produce interference patterns.
  • Optical path length: The effective distance light travels in a medium.

Chapter 41: Relativity

This chapter introduces Einstein's theory of special relativity:

  • Postulates of special relativity: The two postulates upon which the theory is based.
  • Relativistic linear momentum: The momentum of an object at high speeds.
  • Limiting speed: The speed of light as an upper limit.
  • Relativistic energy: The total energy of a body including rest energy.
  • Time dilation: How time appears to slow down for moving objects.
  • Simultaneity: Events that are simultaneous in one frame may not be in another.
  • Length contraction: How lengths appear to contract for moving objects.
  • Velocity addition formula: How velocities add in special relativity.

Chapter 42: Quantum Physics and Wave Mechanics

This chapter introduces the basic principles of quantum mechanics:

  • Quanta of radiation: The energy of a photon is proportional to its frequency (Planck's constant).
  • Photoelectric effect: Emission of electrons from a material when illuminated by light.
  • Momentum of a photon: The momentum of a photon is proportional to its frequency.
  • Compton effect: The change in wavelength of a photon when it scatters off an electron.
  • de Broglie waves: The wave nature of matter.
  • Quantized energies: Energy levels in bound systems.

Chapter 43: The Hydrogen Atom

This chapter applies quantum mechanics to the hydrogen atom:

  • Electron orbits: The allowed energy levels of electrons in hydrogen are examined, using Bohr's model.
  • Energy-level diagrams: Visual representation of energy levels.
  • Emission of light: Photons are emitted when an electron falls from a higher energy level to a lower level. The relationship between energy difference and photon wavelength is discussed.
  • Spectral lines: The discrete wavelengths of light emitted by hydrogen.
  • Absorption of light: Atoms absorb photons only at specific frequencies that correspond to energy level differences.

Chapter 44: Multielectron Atoms

This chapter extends quantum mechanics to atoms with more than one electron:

  • Quantum numbers: The four quantum numbers that describe the state of an electron are introduced.
  • Pauli exclusion principle: No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of quantum numbers.

Chapter 45: Nuclei and Radioactivity

This chapter covers nuclear physics:

  • The nucleus: The structure and properties of the atomic nucleus are explained.
  • Nuclear charge and atomic number: The relationship between nuclear charge, atomic number, and the number of protons.
  • Mass number: The total number of nucleons (protons and neutrons) in a nucleus.
  • Isotopes: Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.
  • Binding energies: The energy required to separate nucleons from a nucleus.
  • Radioactivity: Spontaneous decay of unstable nuclei. Half-life is defined.
  • Nuclear equations: How to write balanced nuclear equations.

Chapter 46: Applied Nuclear Physics

This chapter discusses practical applications of nuclear physics:

  • Nuclear binding energy: The energy that binds nucleons together in a nucleus.
  • Fission reactions: The splitting of heavy nuclei into lighter nuclei, releasing energy.
  • Fusion reactions: Combining light nuclei to form heavier nuclei, releasing energy.
  • Radiation dose: A measure of the amount of radiation absorbed by a material.
  • Radiation damage potential: The damage caused by radiation depends on the type of radiation and the tissue exposed.
  • Effective radiation dose: Considers both the type and biological effects of radiation.
  • High-energy accelerators: Machines used to accelerate charged particles to high energies.
